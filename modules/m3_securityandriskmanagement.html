Security and Risk Management

This module explores how security and risk management intersect, covering assessment methods, threat modelling, and disaster recovery.

Learning Outcomes

Module 3, Security and Risk Management, addressed risk identification and modelling, threat modelling, security and privacy standards, quantitative risk methods, business continuity, and the legal and professional context of information security. The module combined two structured assessments, a team risk identification report for a fictional retailer, Pampered Pets, and an individual quantitative risk model for the same business, with collaborative discussions, seminar preparation, and a peer-delivered debate on emerging trends.

Module learning outcomes:

  • Identify and analyse critically security risks, threats and vulnerabilities in information systems, accounting for the current threat landscape.
  • Gather and synthesise information from multiple sources, including internet security alerts and warning sites, to aid in the systematic analysis of risks and security issues.
  • Critically determine appropriate methodologies, tools and techniques to mitigate and/or solve security risks and their business impact.
  • Articulate the legal, social, ethical and professional issues faced by information security and risk professionals.

Units 1 to 12

Unit 1: An Introduction to Security and Risk Management

Initial post for Collaborative Discussion 1, analysing how data and technology aggravate fairness and accountability risks in the maritime human rights investigations described by Hancock et al. (2024), applying NIST SP 800-30 system characterisation and Hubbard's argument for preserving risk as a two-dimensional quantity. Artefact: initial post.

Unit 2: Users, Assessments and the Risk Management Process

Two peer responses within Collaborative Discussion 1, engaging with fellow students' analyses of the case study and extending them with additional evidence and mitigation perspectives, in line with the peer review guidelines. Artefact: combined forum print containing both response threads, peer names redacted, with VLE permalinks embedded.

Unit 3: Introduction to Threat Modelling and Management

Summary post concluding the three-week discussion, synthesising peer contributions with two dimensions the exchange had not initially surfaced, retaliation risk from compromised investigative data and the legal defensibility of algorithmic evidence, and bridging to structured threat modelling as the methodological answer (Shevchenko et al., 2018). Artefact: summary post.

Unit 4: Application of Threat Modelling and Management Techniques

Written preparation for the seminar on industrial cyber-physical systems, answering three set questions on the elements and interdependencies of a comprehensive ICPS threat model, how threat modelling surfaces attack entry points, and scenario-specific metrics for prioritising countermeasures. Artefact: seminar preparation.

Unit 5: Security and Risk Standards in Industry and the Enterprise

GDPR case study on domestic CCTV and the household exemption, examining the DPC's handling of a neighbour complaint and the Ryneš boundary, with mitigation steps from an Information Security Manager's perspective. Written retrospectively during portfolio assembly, as labelled in the entry itself. Artefact: GDPR case study.

Unit 6: The Practical Implications of Security and Risk Standards

Development Team Project: a risk identification report for Pampered Pets produced with Peer C, applying ISO/IEC 27005 with a qualitative matrix and STRIDE against the proposed e-commerce data flow. Artefact: the team report, with the tutor feedback permalink embedded on its cover page. Evidence: tutor feedback (70%), including verbal feedback that bolder inputs were expected, which shaped the Unit 11 work.

Unit 7: Concepts of Quantitative Risk Modelling

Initial post for Collaborative Discussion 2, assessing the CVSS consistency critique of Wunder et al. (2024), arguing that severity is routinely mistaken for risk, and making the case for the decision-oriented SSVC as a replacement while acknowledging its own validation gap. Artefact: initial post.

Unit 8: Implementing Quantitative Risk Models

Seminar preparation applying attack trees and Markov chains to social engineering threats (Aijaz and Nazir, 2024), challenging the precision-versus-accuracy of assumed inputs, plus two peer responses testing whether SSVC merely relocates rater disagreement to its decision points (Cox, 2008). I also shared with the tutor two public resources from my professional practice: my employer's disruption of the Contagious Interview social engineering campaign, and a defence-in-depth whitepaper on securing digital assets. Artefacts: seminar preparation and peer responses.

Unit 9: Risk, Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery

Summary post concluding Collaborative Discussion 2, revising the initial position: CVSS and EPSS are complementary single-dimension signals, best combined with asset context inside a transparent decision framework, reframing vulnerability prioritisation as a multi-criteria decision problem. Artefact: summary post.

Unit 10: Practical Applications and Issues in DR Implementations

The unit's learning is evidenced through its direct application in the Unit 11 project: the reviewed papers on cloud vendor lock-in (Kumar, 2024) and mission thread analysis (Corbari et al., 2024) inform the executive summary's disaster recovery design against defined RPO and RTO targets and its vendor lock-in mitigations, including explicit exit terms and escrow.

Unit 11: Future Trends in Security and Risk Management

Individual Project: a board-facing executive summary quantifying quality, supply-chain and continuity risks of the international digitalisation programme using FAIR with Monte Carlo simulation, supported by a self-built, fully transparent risk model workbook designed to stand alone as a portfolio artefact. Artefacts: executive summary and workbook.

Unit 12: The Future of SRM

The Great Debate: a joint presentation with Peer A arguing that quantum computing is the most influential SRM trend of the next five years, centred on the harvest now, decrypt later exposure, delivered live in the tutor-led seminar with each presenter covering part of the slides. Artefact: slide deck.

Evaluation: Final Project (Unit 11) vs Status Document (Unit 6)

Note on interpretation: the Unit 6 Risk Identification Report is treated here as the status document, being the module's mid-point team deliverable against the same client scenario.

The two documents assess the same client but reach different conclusions on internationalisation, and the difference is methodological rather than factual. The Unit 6 report, produced as a team, applied ISO/IEC 27005 with a qualitative 5x5 matrix, a choice justified at the time by the absence of incident and loss data (Cox, 2008). Within that method, international expansion appeared to threaten the company's defining asset, consistent product quality, in ways the analysis could not bound, so the team recommended a phased digital transformation while excluding the international supply chain from scope. Precaution was the rational response to unquantified exposure.

The Unit 11 executive summary revisited that exposure with a quantitative method suited to a board committing capital: a FAIR decomposition with Monte Carlo simulation and calibrated PERT inputs. Decomposition made the threat tractable. A high probability of some quality deviation (about 94% per year) proved to be a property of any global chain rather than a verdict on this one; the decision-relevant figures, a roughly 17% annual probability of a recall-grade event and stockout probabilities expressed as a function of buffer size, were manageable once specific controls were funded. The conclusion therefore reversed: the programme is achievable from a risk perspective. What changed was not the facts but the strength of knowledge supporting the assessment (Aven and Thekdi, 2025); quantification converted an unbounded concern into a priced and controllable one.

Two limitations qualify the later document. The growth objective was not modelled, so Unit 11 establishes risk achievability rather than a full investment appraisal, and its inputs are calibrated estimates that await recalibration against real trading data. The comparison itself is the module's central lesson made concrete: the choice between qualitative and quantitative assessment is not a stylistic preference but a determinant of what conclusions become possible.

Reflection

Structured using Rolfe et al.'s (2001) reflective model: What, So what, Now what.
Note: peer names below are anonymised for this public page (Peer A, Peer C); the version submitted for assessment uses real names with a private mapping note.

What

This module was anchored in a single consultancy scenario, Pampered Pets, which was addressed twice: first in Unit 6, where a two-person team produced a qualitative risk identification report, and again in Unit 11, where I individually produced a quantitative executive summary for the board of the digitalised, international business. Two experiences shaped my learning most. The first was the tutor's feedback on the Unit 6 report, which scored 70% but was accompanied by a verbal comment that bolder inputs were expected given my professional experience. The second was my first live academic collaboration, working with a previously unknown teammate, Peer C, across a significant time-zone and cultural distance, followed by a second, contrasting collaboration with Peer A on the Unit 12 debate.

So what

The "by the book" feedback was thought provoking rather than deflating. On reflection, I recognised that I had treated the assignment scope as a boundary rather than a floor, deliberately setting aside several years of industry experience in order to stay safely inside the brief. The irony is that I had cited Hubbard (2020), whose central argument is that qualitative risk matrices conceal more than they reveal, while choosing exactly such a matrix myself. The choice was defensible; Cox (2008) warns against the misleading precision of quantification without data, and a small retailer with no incident history fits that warning. What the feedback exposed was that defensibility and commitment are not the same thing, and that playing safe was itself a decision with a cost.

Unit 11 became my deliberate response. Building a FAIR-based Monte Carlo model forced me into sustained discomfort. Pet food is not an industry I know, and having to define loss frequencies and magnitudes without domain knowledge initially felt like a weakness I was exposing rather than a method I was applying. What resolved this was a change in how I understood boldness: not confidence in the numbers, but willingness to commit to calibrated estimates while stating their weakness openly. I anchored inputs to published data points, used PERT ranges rather than single figures, and recommended recalibration as the business generates its own data, which is the approach Aven and Thekdi (2025) prescribe where the knowledge supporting a risk assessment is weak. The hardest part was tonal: presenting a persuasive case to an investor without drifting into optimism. Holding that balance, a strong recommendation resting visibly on stated assumptions, was the most demanding exercise of the module and the one that changed my practice most.

The feedback had a second, unexpected effect. It pushed Peer A and me to choose quantum computing for the Unit 12 debate, a topic the module had not covered. It later emerged that the tutor had intended to introduce quantum computing in that same seminar, which I took as a proof point that the open-minded posture the feedback demanded was the right one.

The team experience produced a different kind of learning. The first sessions with Peer C were awkward in a way I had not anticipated: challenging the inputs of someone I did not know, and being challenged in return, felt uncomfortable before any working relationship existed. Over four sessions of alternating writer and reviewer roles this eased considerably, a progression consistent with Tuckman's (1965) movement from forming toward performing. What I noticed about myself is that I compensated for the missing rapport with process: I initiated contact, fixed the schedule, and followed up on every session. Process held the collaboration together while trust was still forming, but it was Peer C's insistence on leaving no grey area, questioning the rationale behind every choice, that raised the quality of the report. The contrast with the Peer A collaboration was instructive. Sharing a regional context removed much of the adaptation effort and made coordination faster, yet the harder, more culturally distant collaboration was the one that stretched my thinking further. In a deliberately multicultural cohort, I now see that friction and enrichment tend to arrive together.

Now what

Three changes follow from this module, one already applied. First, the quantitative approach is already in use: the Unit 11 model itself evidences the shift from safe qualitative scoring to committed, transparent estimation, and the method transfers directly to my professional context in digital assets, where I can express security exposure to stakeholders in financial terms rather than colour codes. The debate topic transfers equally directly, since harvest now, decrypt later is a live concern for any custodian of long-lived encrypted material, and I am now equipped to argue its urgency quantitatively. Second, in future distributed teams I will build the working relationship deliberately and early, agreeing communication norms and scope in the first session rather than letting rapport accumulate as a by-product of process; process should scaffold trust, not substitute for it. Third, and most fundamentally, I have changed how I handle uncertainty in front of decision makers. My instinct was to withhold judgement where data was thin; I will instead commit to calibrated estimates, expose their assumptions plainly, and build recalibration into the recommendation. Boldness, I have learned, is not the opposite of rigour; stating clearly what you do not know is what makes bold inputs defensible.

Team Meeting Notes

Coordination for the Unit 6 team project was conducted through scheduled calls across a significant time-zone gap, with four working sessions in total, and follow-up by email between sessions. No formal minutes were kept; the working method was an alternating writer and reviewer role each session, with the report itself, and the progressive changes between drafts, standing as the practical record of what each session resolved. Coordination for the Unit 12 debate with Peer A followed the same informal pattern: a small number of calls plus email exchanges to align the argument and divide the slides, made easier by both presenters working in the same regional time zone.

Feedback from Peers and Tutors

Tutor feedback on the Unit 6 Risk Identification Report: 70%, with written and verbal feedback that the analysis was competent but that bolder inputs were expected given my professional experience. This feedback is embedded as a permalink on the cover page of the report itself (see Unit 6 artefact above) and is discussed at length in the Reflection and in the Unit 6 vs Unit 11 evaluation, since it directly shaped the methodology of the Unit 11 project.

Peer feedback on the Unit 6 project was exchanged through the platform's peer evaluation process; the substance of that exchange, on the value of leaving no grey area unquestioned, is reflected in the Reflection above rather than reproduced here, since peer evaluations are not intended for wider circulation.
