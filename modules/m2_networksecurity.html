Network Security

An exploration of network vulnerabilities, security assessments, and mitigation strategies.

Learning Outcomes

This module focused on the principles and practical implementation of network security, vulnerability assessment, and risk based security management. The content explored how modern network infrastructures introduce complex attack surfaces and require layered defensive strategies combining technical controls, governance frameworks, and operational monitoring.

Knapp, E.D. (2024) Industrial Network Security. 3rd edn. [Online].

The module examined key concepts including:

  • Network architecture and attack surfaces
  • Vulnerability assessment and penetration testing
  • Cyber kill chain and attack lifecycle models
  • Advanced persistent threats and supply chain attacks
  • Risk based security decision making

Industrial and enterprise networks increasingly rely on interconnected systems, cloud services, and operational technology, which expands the attack surface and introduces new security risks that must be continuously assessed and mitigated (Knapp, 2024).

Knapp, E.D. (2024) Industrial Network Security. 3rd edn. [Online].

By completing this module, I developed the ability to:

  • Perform structured vulnerability assessments
  • Analyse network security risks using recognised frameworks
  • Translate technical findings into business risk decisions
  • Evaluate security controls and mitigation strategies
  • Apply academic and industry cybersecurity models to real world scenarios

Artefacts

Mid Module Technical Assessment - Security Risk Identification and Technical Design Analysis

This artefact consisted of a technical vulnerability assessment of the Acunetix Acuart environment. The assessment included reconnaissance, vulnerability scanning, risk prioritisation, and mitigation planning.

The assessment utilised a multi layered approach including:

  • Kali Linux virtual machine
  • Passive reconnaissance tools such as traceroute, dig, and whois
  • Tenable Nessus vulnerability scanning
  • OWASP Top 10 mapping

The analysis identified critical vulnerabilities including unsupported PHP versions, injection risks, and outdated components. These findings were prioritised based on exploitability and business impact, demonstrating a structured risk based approach.

Tutor feedback highlighted strong technical analysis and structured methodology. The report demonstrated effective vulnerability identification and clear risk prioritisation. The use of academic and industry frameworks also strengthened the professional quality of the assessment.

This artefact demonstrated my ability to conduct technical vulnerability assessments and communicate findings in a structured and professional manner.

End Module Assessment - Executive Summary and Critical Reflection

The final assessment required synthesising technical findings into a professional executive summary supported by a critical reflection.

The executive summary focused on:

  • Legacy PHP vulnerabilities
  • Unsupported software risks
  • Injection vulnerabilities
  • Lack of WAF and input validation
  • Third party supply chain risk

The analysis incorporated the Cyber Kill Chain framework to illustrate how attackers could exploit vulnerabilities across multiple stages. The Cyber Kill Chain model breaks cyber attacks into phases including reconnaissance, exploitation, and command and control, allowing organisations to detect and disrupt threats earlier in the attack lifecycle (Kidd, 2022).

Kidd, C. (2022) Cyber Kill Chains: Strategies & Tactics.

The final assessment also considered modern cyber threats such as supply chain attacks. The SolarWinds breach demonstrated how attackers can compromise trusted software updates and gain access to thousands of organisations, highlighting the importance of third party risk management and continuous monitoring.

Temple-Raston, D. (2021) ‘A “Worst Nightmare” Cyberattack: The Untold Story Of The SolarWinds Hack’, All Things Considered, 16 April.
Case Study: The SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack – A Wake-Up Call for Supply Chain Security (2024) 20 November.

Tutor feedback highlighted strong vulnerability prioritisation and logical remediation planning. However, the feedback suggested that recommendations could be more tightly integrated into technical design and that the reflection would benefit from a clearer technical development roadmap.

This assessment strengthened my ability to translate technical vulnerabilities into strategic security recommendations.

Reflection

Module 2 strengthened my understanding of network security as both a technical and risk driven discipline. While the mid module assessment focused on vulnerability identification, the final assessment emphasised the importance of integrating technical findings into broader risk management and governance decisions.

A key learning point was recognising that cybersecurity prioritisation should be based on threat likelihood, vulnerability exposure, and business impact, rather than technical severity alone (Knapp, 2024).

Knapp, E.D. (2024) Industrial Network Security. 3rd edn. [Online].

The module also improved my understanding of modern threats such as advanced persistent threats and supply chain attacks, highlighting the need for layered security controls and continuous monitoring.

Alshamrani, A., Myneni, S., Chowdhary, A. and Huang, D. (2019) ‘A Survey on Advanced Persistent Threats: Techniques, Solutions, Challenges, and Research Opportunities’, IEEE Communications Surveys & Tutorials, 21(2), pp. 1851–1877.
Temple-Raston, D. (2021) ‘A “Worst Nightmare” Cyberattack: The Untold Story Of The SolarWinds Hack’, All Things Considered, 16 April.

Overall, this module improved my practical skills in vulnerability assessment, risk prioritisation, and professional communication, strengthening my ability to apply risk based decision making in network security environments.

Key Takeaways

This combined feedback highlighted the importance of strengthening the connection between vulnerability identification, technical design, and professional development planning. These insights will guide my continued development in future modules.

Professional Skills Matrix and Action Plan

To address this, I plan to deepen my practical experience in vulnerability assessment, penetration testing, and network analysis. I also aim to improve how I communicate technical risks in a clear and structured way, particularly when linking technical issues to business impact.

These improvements will support my continued development throughout the MSc programme and strengthen my practical cybersecurity skills.
